Introduction

The FreeCodeCamp Visual Web Design Certificate consists of 5 projects, each with set requirements expressed as User Stories. For each project, you’re given a project concept and a set of requirements to fulfil, alongside a snippet of JavaScript to test if you’ve met all of the constraints. Once you’re done with each project, you submit it to FreeCodeCamp, and after completing all 5, a certificate is generated in your name — hosted on the FreeCodeCamp site in perpetuity.

Completing and submitting these projects was a pretty great experience — it gave me a chance to build 5 different websites with defined constraints, practice my coding skills and most importantly, design 5 different websites. Each page has a distinct brand identity, layout and overall design purpose. For someone looking to transition into digital Product Design, this seemed like a great chance to stretch my skills and ship some projects that had some practical goals, rather than fake concepts in my design files.

In total, I built 5 projects. For each project, I fulfilled the FreeCodeCamp constraints — while adding my own goals on top of that. This case study will talk about those constraints, my overall goals, and the outcomes for each project.

In general, building these pages was a great experience. While these aren't Products with a capital P (with users, customers or business models), I did my best to treat them as realistically as possible. Throughout these projects, I got great exposure designing to user stories, managing compromises, and the experience of what actually deploying live websites is like.

5 Biggest Lessons:

Throughout these 5 websites, the lessons I learnt included design, coding, writing, project management and just general planning. The most important lessons I want to focus on are all orientated around design, especially related to current industry trends.

Although I haven’t worked in the technology industry, I follow the tech/design scene closely. That mostly looks like a lot of time spent on Twitter, podcasts and blog posts. Because of that, I know a considerable focus in design right now (2019) is big (and growing) investments in design systems. From what I’ve seen, that shift helps people move their focus away from designing individual boxes in Sketch/Figma/Photoshop/XD to freeing up time for solving real customer, people and business problems. This quote, by Jina, courtesy of XYZ, really helps frame this shift.

With design systems in mine; these 5 projects gave me a deep appreciation for the benefits, purpose and need for design systems. Looking at my summarised lessons, there are specific themes of structure, planning, consistency, and functionality. I think these ideas closely mirror the benefits design systems provide and for nothing else these are some great lessons to learn. I’m super excited to dive into design systems in the future and keep learning from industry experts paving the way!

  1. Set the project up for success from the start. That means always using responsive and fluid design principles, establishing base grid systems early, and using system-design for colours, layout and typography.
  2. The most important product is the final deployed design, having a cool idea in a design tool loses its shine if it makes the product more difficult to use or not functional.
  3. Organise everything in a usable, clear way. This helps you keep working on and adding additional design functionality as you progress.
  4. With each different 'brand identity', each project has similar base foundations, especially in Typography, Layout and Spacing.
  5. Use a step by step process by starting with core functionality, then adding additional styles, layout and structure at each step. Think about the core goal of the product, build that function, then add on from there with required extensions.

These lessons came up to varying degrees throughout each project — and will definitely help me in making more consistent and enjoyable product experiences in the future.

Applying a Consistent Design Process

Each of the 5 projects followed a consistent design process — and one that slightly differed from my usual method. With more typical design problems, I would explore potential solutions, do extensive research, ask questions about the problem we’re solving and identify key stakeholders to consult work alongside before committing directly to a solution.

For these projects — the solution (a website) was defined, and this was more experience in design delivery than exploration. Within that context, every project was kicked off by looking at the required user stories and building from that point onwards. This process ensured the required user stories were completed — and helped structure the extended user experiences around the necessary functionality, rather than building beautiful presentation layers and adding functionality in backwards. This basics first approach was definitely useful, and mirror similar MVP style product development approaches.

Goals and Overall Approach

Within each goal, I had some direction provided by the technical constraints of FreeCodeCamp, and then I had my own goals for each project — which focused on usability and user experience.

Within each project, the majority of the FreeCodeCamp requirements were technically completed (as judged by FreeCodeCamp) with HTML markup, and very minimal styles via CSS. This resulted in barebones pages — missing opinionated typography, clear hierarchy or apparent structure.

That barebones presentation wasn’t quite the desirable, visually appealing functional solutions I was aiming for as I treated these as serious ‘fake’ projects. Because of that, I made sure that once each test was passed, I kept refining and iterating on each project to build desirable, approachable user experiences layered on top of that foundational functional layer.

Defining Design Constraints

Counterbalancing the technical goals provided by FreeCodeCamp — each project had a few additional constraints that I held myself too. These can most easily be explained as either A) Design Best Practices or B) Pushing myself out of my comfort zone.

Design Constraints:

  • Fully responsive and fluid layouts for any potential screensizes.
  • Semantic HTML5 whenever possible to maintain accessibility and keep websites as modern as possible.
  • A focus on only HTML5 and CSS with limited to no JavaScript or other build tools. This helped ensure speed and perfomance.
  • A unique brand identity for every page, ensuring every page has a different style, personality and layout structure.
  • Original copywriting for every page, reflecting that copy and product writing is an essential part of user experience.

Project 1: Designing a Tribute Page

View the page at A Tribute to 200 years of Technological Innovators

This project focuses on a dynamic grid-based layout, with the alternating images and text pattern creating visual hierarchy and interest.

To remain responsive, the images fluidly respond to browser size changes — at a certain point shrinking to fit in a small screen view.

The image, title and text component used here is duplicated throughout each innovators description, with CSS being used to manipulate position. This reduced duplication and ensured the same components could be used throughout.

Main Focuses:

  • Hierarchy
  • Fluid images
  • Alternating grid layout

Tiny Polish:

Hovering over each image slightly increases the size of the image and activates the link cursor icon — showing the image itself is a clickable link.

What I could improve next time:

For future similar projects, I would explore ways to generate each component programmatically using a content management system, markdown generator or build tool generator. This would reduce the manual duplication of each element and could introduce features such as dynamic ordering or user-submitted content while ensuring a consistent display approach.

Project 2: Designing a Sign-up Form

Sign up* to the site at Mastering Forms
*Don’t actually sign-up, this is a fake project!

Web forms are notorious for being tricky to work with, style and design. For this project, I focused on doing the basics well. Using modern HTML5, I was able to clearly lay out each form section, attach clear labels to each question, and add small affordances to help the user progress through the form.

The topographic background (courtesy of http://www.heropatterns.com/) helps set a friendly, welcoming tone to the website.

On the small screen view, form elements rearrange to ensure usability and clear separation between different components, helping users to see which answers relate to which questions.

Main Focuses:

  • Responsive forms
  • Clear labels and product copy
  • Effective form layout and hierarchy

Tiny Polish:

The required questions turn green/red when they have met the validation criteria. Try it out! The validation itself is handled with HTML5.

What I could improve next time:

Further styling of the checkboxes and radio buttons could significantly improve the polish of the website, taking it to that next level of personalisation. However, this would need to be carefully balanced with accessibility concerns.

Additionally, this particular sign-up form product could be enhanced by either illustrations or photos and quotes — additional ways to add social proof and show a welcoming, inclusive culture.

Project 3: Designing a Product Landing page

Visit the landing page at Adding Spice to Life: Product Landing Page

Landing page design has a very tried and true history. Styles have trended in and out of fashion, with the current trend illustration focused. The FreeCodeCamp project required a video, which provided the core media element for the top of the page.

To introduce variety, I experimented with bold borders and a semi-chaotic grid for the favourite recipes section. This created a bit of a broken grid feel while retaining an underlying orderly grid that everything is structured around.

Overall, this project needs a bit more work to be genuinely polished — I think in part showing the gap between my initial ‘concept’, and the final design I actually delivered. Good experience in continuing to work on my craft and design execution skills.

Main Focuses:

  • Hierarchy
  • Breaking the Grid
  • Bold layout

Tiny Polish:

The copywriting for this page adds lots of personality, particularly the captions for the favourite recipes.

What I could improve next time:

Re-visit the sizing, layout and margins between different elements. The initial concept was to have overlapping features, however, while iterating, I couldn’t make that work — especially from a contrast and readability perspective. Once that was abandoned, the elements should have been shrunken a little, as they are currently slightly large.

The navigation bar was fixed to fulfil a FreeCodeCamp requirement — however, it could have been made a little smaller, so it took up less focus and attention. New icons or small design elements may have helped to make it more appealing, as it currently stands out pleasantly.

Project 4: Designing a Technical Documentation Page

See the Seed CSS site at Seed: Utility CSS Code Snippets

This was the most exciting and ambitious project by a significant margin and the biggest learning opportunity across all of 5 projects. During this project, I spent a lot of time thinking about designing a system to be flexible, reusable and practical. Coming from outside the Design industry — this was a great prompt to think deeply about how to design-systems could be used, why they can be useful and start to understand some of the complexity and issues that need to be addressed.

While the initial concept for this project was creating code snippets I could reuse myself in the future — I (inadvertently) ended up spending a lot of time thinking about functional CSS, design components, product consistency and design reusability.

On top of that, this project was the most complex of all 5 in terms of navigation, information architecture, layout, hierarchy and usability. Each separate section had to be easy to quickly understand, navigate and use.

Main Focuses:

  • Systems-thinking
  • Hierarchy
  • Balancing responsive design with a lot of content
  • Effective navigation
  • Clear structure
  • Strong layout
  • Clear copy and product writing

Tiny Polish:

The code block elements are sized based on the content, up until a certain point. At that point, they become scroll-able, with the content constrained within that element. This keeps things consistent while also resizing for the material as possible.

As you scroll through the page, the components navigation bar highlights the currently active section. Unfortunately — this doesn’t quite always work, but overall I’m a big fan of this effect.

Tiny Polish:

When navigating with the cards on the left, the content on the right uses ‘smooth’ scroll behaviour, a CSS property that scrolls the page down to the relevant section, rather than instantly moving the screen to the correct position.

This helps the user realise this is a single page scrolling website, rather than multiple pages.

What I could improve next time:

Similar to project 1, programmatically creating each card could improve consistency, reduce manual repetition and overall efficiency.

Results and Outcomes

Every single one of these projects forced me to consider trade-offs and make decisions between different design patterns. Compared to designing these pages in Figma (my design tool of choice), these websites had real constraints and requirements.

All 5 projects were intentionally designed with specific outcomes in mind — with each project to more or less success. All learning opportunities! One of the explicit constraints I held tightly was that everything had to be fully responsive, working as well on an iPhone 5 size screen as on a laptop or desktop. Every project met that goal, to different extents. And having that goal forced me to design and build better and better at each project.

As a result, I’m confident the next project I build going forward will be more intentional, designed with more structure and based around an earlier defined design system.

Overall, the 5 lessons of setting projects up with strong foundations from the start, focusing on the final delivered design, organising everything for continued progression, maintaining consistent brand identity and using a step by step process of functionality first and expression afterwards will set me up for success in the future.

The lessons from these projects will carry me forward to continue doing better and better design in the future.

Next Steps

I started this project to work on visual and interaction design. My professional experience (link to my CV) has focused on a lot of user/customer/service experience design, with a lot of time spent using post-its and less time on visual design.

This certificate gave me a great chance to keep practising my visual design execution and overall design craft — but it’s time to shift back towards solving user experience problems through the full product journey.

My continued focus will be working through the entire product design process; doing discovery, research, problem definition, ideation and then finally solution design.
